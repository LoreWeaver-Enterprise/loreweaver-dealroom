How Director Makes Money

Director generates revenue through two complementary streams.

Stream 1: Royalty Model (primary)

Studios integrate Director into their game during development at no cost. Once the shipped game earns more than €100K in annual revenue, LoreWeaver collects a 1% royalty on all revenue above that threshold. This follows the same "free to use, pay on success" structure as Unreal Engine, but at a significantly lower rate (1% vs Unreal's 5%) and a lower threshold (€100K vs Unreal's $1M lifetime).

This model aligns incentives perfectly. LoreWeaver earns more only when the studio earns more. Studios face zero upfront cost and zero risk during development. The €100K threshold ensures small indie titles never pay a cent, and the 1% rate is far more studio-friendly than industry comparables.

Stream 2: Seat-Based Licensing (alternative)

Studios that prefer predictable costs can opt for a flat license at €10,000/month plus add-ons. This appeals to large publishers running multiple titles who want budget certainty. It also provides LoreWeaver with stable baseline revenue independent of game performance.

Why revenue starts in Year 3 (2028)

Director is a runtime engine embedded in shipped games. The development cycle matters: a studio integrating Director in 2026 ships their game in 2027 or 2028. Only then does that game generate revenue, and only above the €100K threshold does LoreWeaver collect royalties. This lag is inherent to the B2B game middleware model and matches the ramp profile Unreal Engine experienced in its early years.

Key Mechanics

  • The €100K threshold resets annually. Each calendar year, the first €100K is royalty-free.
  • Royalties compound across titles. A studio with 5 games each earning €20M pays royalties on all five.
  • Live-service games (MMOs, GaaS) generate recurring royalties year after year, creating compounding revenue streams from each integration.

Pricing Options

Option Structure Best For LoreWeaver Upside
Royalty Free during dev; 1% above €100K/yr Studios building high-revenue titles; studios wanting zero upfront cost Uncapped; scales with game success
Seat License €10,000/month flat + add-ons Large publishers with multiple titles; budget-sensitive procurement Predictable; not tied to game performance

Crossover point: The seat-based license (€120K/year) costs less than the royalty model when a single title earns above ~€12.1M/year. Studios with very high-revenue games save money on seat licenses. Studios with uncertain revenue prefer the royalty model because it costs nothing if the game underperforms.

Both options include the same Director runtime, SDK, engine plugins, and documentation. The difference is purely commercial.

Why 91% gross margin is sustainable:

Director runs entirely on the player's hardware. Unlike cloud-based AI services (which incur GPU inference costs per request), Director's local model executes on the CPU/GPU already in the player's PC or console. This means:

  • No cloud compute scaling costs as player counts grow.
  • No per-session API fees that erode margin at scale.
  • COGS consists only of support staff, QA testing for new integrations, and engineering resources for studio onboarding.
  • As the customer base grows, COGS scales sub-linearly because integration patterns become standardized.

This margin profile is comparable to traditional middleware (Wwise, Havok) and significantly better than cloud AI services (which typically run 40 to 60% gross margins due to inference costs).
